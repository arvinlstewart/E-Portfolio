Why I Take One to Two Hours to Strategize Before Campaign Setup

By Arvin Stewart | Published on May 1, 2025

Before the first bid is placed, the real campaign begins.

There’s this adrenaline most campaign managers know too well — the pressure to launch quickly. Clients want performance yesterday. Timelines shrink. Budgets appear out of thin air. But no matter how urgent the campaign, I carve out one to two hours before anything gets built. No line items. No tags. Just strategy. Thought. Foresight.

Early in my career, I thought speed was value. I would open the DSP, plug in what I was given, and hit launch. But then came the questions:
- Why isn’t this performing?
- Why are we pacing too quickly in one region and not another?
- Why is the CTR fine but conversions tanking?

Those questions didn’t have answers — not because the data wasn’t available, but because I hadn’t taken time to set expectations and define what success actually looked like.

Strategic setup reduces technical debt.
If a campaign is built in haste, it needs fixing later — restructuring budgets, rebuilding creatives, toggling optimization goals. Each of those mid-flight changes creates noise in the data. But when I block off time to plan things like targeting logic, KPI alignment, pacing strategy, naming conventions, and budget splits — I build with purpose, not panic.

Naming conventions are not trivial.
Clean naming = clean data. It’s the first thing I tackle. And I don’t do it manually anymore — I use a dynamic generator I built in Excel to structure names by platform, audience, creative, and more. It ensures consistency, saves time, and supports faster QA.

Strategy also lets me challenge unclear requests.
When I’ve taken time to assess the big picture, I can say things like:

  • “This KPI won’t be trackable without a better conversion tag.”
  • “Budget might be spread too thin across all these platforms.”
  • “These creatives are sized wrong for in-feed native.”
That builds trust with clients. It’s not pushback — it’s thought partnership.

The launch becomes a confirmation — not a gamble.
Once I launch, I know I’ve already sanity-checked every element. My dashboards light up with confidence. If anything underperforms, I can isolate it fast — and not waste 48 hours diagnosing what should’ve been caught before go-live.

In short: strategy isn’t extra. It’s essential.
A few hours of focus before setup saves you from dozens of hours in cleanup, excuses, and apologies later. I’ve learned that the hard way — and now, I make it my standard.
